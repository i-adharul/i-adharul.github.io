Part 1 - Olist business condition & customer segmentation

About the data and the project

This project uses data that has been shared by Olist, Dabague, and Francisco Magioli on Kaggle, you can see the data here.

It is explained that this data is a Brazilian ecommerce public dataset of orders made at Olist Store. There are also a few other paragraphs explaining what this olist actually is, but it still seems quite confusing. So I finally searched the web, here, to find out the context and business model of Olist.

So in short, Olist is a SaaS company that helps SMEs to sell in many e-commerce sites in Brazil and America with only one registration. With Olist, SMEs do not need to create multiple accounts in many e-commerce sites. However, the account name used to sell in each of these e-commerce is Olist Store. So basically, these SMEs display their merchandise on the Olist Store account while getting several other services such as consulting product displays and ads, adjusting product prices, reporting dashboards, product delivery management, lower courier costs, stock management, etc.

From an understanding of Olist and its business model, we can see that there are many interesting things that can be explored, starting from the aspect of sellers, consumers, products sold, marketing, delivery of goods, etc.

If you look at the description of the data on Kaggle, there is no specific problem to solve with this data. Therefore, in this project, I will first explore the dataset to understand first the condition of Olist's business and what has happened in Olist at this time. Then dig deeper into the problem and recommend solutions that can improve Olist's business aspects.

Olist business condition

This section only shows what has been and is happening at Olist until August 2018 so that we know where the problem lies or what aspects of the business we should or can improve on.

Based on their website, one of Olist's revenues comes from commissions on the sale of its users' products (seller). Therefore, one of the important metrics to look at is revenue itself and number of orders. As shown in the graph, revenue and number of orders have a similar trend, that is an increase of more than 9 times until November 2017 but stagnant and tends to decrease until August 2018. On the other hand, the trend of the average order review score of Olist during 2017 to 2018 actually decreased and then increased sharply from March 2018 to August 2018.

Now lets move a little bit deeper. In terms of order cancellations due to product unavailability, it shows a downward trend and in terms of average delivery time until the product reaches the customer, it also decreases. Both of these things show good results for Olist, which may also be quite influential for the increase in the order review score which increased in mid-2018.

Even though this is good for Olist, the average delivery time and review score, which are experiencing an increasing trend, still have some notes that need further attention, because there are still orders that have arrived in more than 1 month, and there are still customers who give a rating score below 3.

Back to revenue, here is the distribution of revenue when viewed from several different aspects.

First, in terms of payment type. The majority of payment types used by customers are credit cards. And there has been a slight shift in the use of the payment method from 2017 and 2018. Namely, credit card and debit card users are increasing, while boleto has decreased and vouchers are no longer available.

Second, from the Customer State side, 80% of revenue only comes from 7 states where 60% of revenue comes from the top 3 states, namely Sao Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, and Minas Gerasi. This also applies to revenue per customer city, all of the top 10 cities come from the top ten states.

Third, from the seller's point of view. In contrast to customer cities, sellers who contribute to revenue, 65% come from one state, namely Sao Paulo. Then followed by Minsa Gerais and Parana with a percentage of 8% each.

Finally, in terms of product categories. Although the data looks fairly even for each category, if you look further, house wares and furniture are the products that contribute the most revenue.

Now move to the next metrics apart from revenue, that is the customer cohort. From the graffict, we can see that only less than 2 percent of customers buy more than once through Olist. The rest only buy items from Olist once. And this has a huge impact on Olist's customer segmentation.
